Intro
Why This Matters
Establish the cultural frame before any phrases.
1 / 6
⏱
Suggested time for this block
3–4 min
Set framing before diving into content
💬 Talking Points
›
These phrases aren't just vocabulary — they're social rituals . The Japanese workplace runs on acknowledgment. Help them see this isn't etiquette for etiquette's sake — it keeps a team cohesive.
›
Key framing line to say out loud: "English doesn't have these. That's not because the concept doesn't exist — it's because we express it differently, usually without words."
›
Ask the group: "Has anyone ever had a Japanese colleague say something to them and not known how to respond?" Let one or two people share.
›
Tell them: the goal today isn't memorization — it's knowing when to say something, and why it matters when you do.
👁 Watch For
⚠️
Some learners may feel this is overly formal for a manufacturing context. Acknowledge that directly — "I know this feels ceremonial. But even a small nod and the right word takes two seconds and lands as genuine respect."
💡
If the group seems skeptical, ask: "What do you currently do when a Japanese colleague nods at you and says something?" The answer is usually nothing — and that's the gap this session addresses.
📖 Block A — Deepen
Block A · 6–7 min · Push to unlock on all learner screens
2 / 6
Block A
Deepen — Otsukaresama & Yoroshiku
They've used these before. Now add the register ladder and handoff meaning.
2 / 6
⏱
Suggested time for this block
6–7 min
They know these — go deeper, not wider
💬 Talking Points
›
Start with what they already know: "You've said otsukaresama. Today you learn when desu vs. deshita matters — and why."
›
The desu/deshita distinction is the key unlock: desu = shift still happening, deshita = shift done or task complete. Ask them to think back — which one have they been using?
›
For yoroshiku: this is the moment to reframe it from a greeting to a handoff phrase . "I'm counting on you to handle this well." It's not just nice-to-meet-you.
›
Point them to the register ladder in the table. Have them read all three forms aloud: yoroshiku / yoroshiku onegaishimasu / yoroshiku onegai itashimasu. Ask: "When would you use the formal one?"
›
Spend 2 minutes on the back-chain drill for osaki ni if they haven't done Block B yet — but Block A can stand alone.
👁 Watch For
⚠️
Learners sometimes use otsukare (casual short form) first because it sounds easier. Flag this: "Wait until a Japanese colleague uses it with you first — hearing it is a milestone that means they're comfortable with you."
💡
The culture note on otsukaresama as a closer (not just a greeting) often surprises people. Pause on that. "You can say it when you step away from someone's workstation. It wraps up the interaction warmly."

Block B · 8–10 min · Push to unlock on all learner screens
3 / 6
Block B
New Phrases
Osaki ni, osewa ni narimashita, gokurousama. Each has a story.
3 / 6
⏱
Suggested time for this block
8–10 min
This is the heaviest content block — budget extra time
💬 Talking Points
›
Go phrase by phrase — don't rush. Each one has a cultural reason it exists.
›
For osaki ni shitsurei shimasu : act it out. "Imagine everyone's still at their station. You grab your bag. What do you say?" Pause. Let them read it. Have them say it.
›
For osewa ni narimashita : set up a scenario. "A Japanese engineer has been here for two weeks. Today is their last day. They say this to you. What do you say back?" (kochira koso / mata yoroshiku)
›
For
›
Have them practice the back-chain drill for osaki ni — it's in their learner view. Walk them through it step by step.
👁 Watch For
⚠️
Gokurousama is the most common hierarchy error in this session. Some learners will want to say it back to a manager who says it to them. Be very direct: "This flows downward only. Saying it upward is like a junior employee praising their boss — it lands wrong."
⚠️
Learners may confuse osewa ni natte imasu (ongoing) with osewa ni narimashita (completed). Note: osewa ni natte imasu was removed from this session's active vocab — if it comes up, acknowledge it but don't drill it.
💡
The osaki ni culture note often generates good discussion. "Have you ever just left without saying anything while your Japanese colleagues were still working?" Let them reflect.
🔧 Block C — X は Y です
Block C · 7–8 min · Push to unlock on all learner screens
4 / 6
Block C
X は Y です — The Pattern
Desu is a polite period, not a verb. The equation makes everything click.
4 / 6
⏱
Suggested time for this block
7–8 min
One pattern only — don't open the wa vs. ga door
💬 Talking Points
›
Lead with the framing: "Desu does not mean 'is.' Stop trying to translate it. Think of it as a polite period . It says: sentence done, I'm being polite."
›
Show the equation on their screen: X は + Y + です。 Walk through it slowly. Topic marker は (wa) — what you're talking about. Y — what you say about it. Desu — polite period.
›
Connect it to what they already know: "'[Name] desu' at the end of your self-intro? That IS this pattern. You've been using it since Session 3."
›
Let them try the sentence builder. Give them 3–4 minutes to click through combinations. Tell them some combinations will be flagged as odd or wrong — that's intentional.
›
Key thing to land: the o-namae distinction . "If you add o- to namae, you're adding respect — but only use it for someone ELSE's name, not your own."
👁 Watch For
⚠️
Do NOT introduce wa vs. ga here. If someone asks, say: "That's a great question and there's a real answer — but it's Session 8 material. For now, は is your topic marker and it works in every sentence you need today."
⚠️
Some learners will want to know why は is pronounced "wa" not "ha." Keep it brief: "Japanese uses は as a special particle — it's always read as wa in this role. File it as a quirk and move on."
💡
The sentence builder's ❌ Doesn't Work results teach more than the ✅ Natural ones. Encourage learners to try odd combinations intentionally.
🔄 Block D — もう一度
Block D · 6–7 min · Push to unlock on all learner screens
5 / 6
Block D
もう一度 — Mou Ichido
The phrase that prevents nod-along miscommunication.
5 / 6
⏱
Suggested time for this block
6–7 min
Back-chain drill is the core activity here
💬 Talking Points
›
Frame the problem first: "Has anyone ever nodded along in a conversation and had no idea what was just said?" Let them laugh. "That's the nod-along. It creates real miscommunication on a production floor."
›
Introduce mou ichido onegaishimasu : one more time please. Walk through why onegaishimasu connects to yoroshiku onegaishimasu — same polite ask word, different context.
›
Run the back-chain drill together. Display it on their screens. You say step 1, they repeat. Build to the full phrase. This should feel like a chant, not a recitation.
›
Then walk through the recovery sequence: sumimasen → kiite mo ii desu ka → mou ichido → o-namae wa. Have them read each step aloud.
›
Key message: "It is not rude to ask again. It's more respectful to ask than to nod and get it wrong."
👁 Watch For
⚠️
Some learners will feel embarrassed about asking for repetition. Address this head-on: "Your Japanese colleagues would much rather you ask than nod and then do the wrong thing. Asking is professional."
💡
If time allows, do a live role-play: you say a name (or make one up), the learner misses it, walks through the recovery sequence to ask again. Keep it light.
🙏 Block E — The Bow
Block E · 3–4 min · Push to unlock on all learner screens
6 / 6
Block E
The Bow — Non-Verbal Layer
Words without body language are half the message.
6 / 6
⏱
Suggested time for this block
3–4 min
Keep it light — this closes Phase 2
💬 Talking Points
›
Say this exactly: "You don't need to perform Japan. You need to not accidentally signal indifference ."
›
The only bow they need: a small head nod — 10 to 15 degrees. That's it. Combined with otsukaresama or osaki ni, it lands as genuine.
›
Ask: "What do you currently do when a Japanese colleague bows to you?" Most answers will be some version of nothing. "A nod back takes half a second. It says: I see you."
›
Close with: "These small gestures say I respect this space. That's the whole goal."
👁 Watch For
💡
Some learners will overcorrect and try to do a deep formal bow. Gently correct: "Manufacturing floor, not a formal dinner. Small nod is exactly right."
💡
This is a good moment to ask: "After today, what's the one phrase you're most likely to actually use this week?" Let a few people share. It cements the learning.
