Unit 1 — Acknowledgment Rituals
Why This Matters
Before a single new phrase — here's the frame that makes everything else land.
The Core Idea
Japanese workplace culture has phrases that function like social glue — they aren't about exchanging information. They're about acknowledging each other as people who are working hard together.

English doesn't have clean equivalents. That's why these feel awkward to translate but feel completely natural in use. The goal this session isn't memorization — it's understanding when they're called for and why they matter.

🏭 On お先に失礼します
Leaving before colleagues in Japanese workplace culture carries mild social weight — you're aware you're going first. This phrase says "excuse me for heading out before you." It's considerate rather than apologetic, but it matters. Grabbing your bag and leaving without a word can accidentally signal indifference. This phrase costs nothing and lands well every time.
⚠️ Hierarchy — Gokurousama
This phrase flows downward only — manager to worker. If a supervisor says it to you, receive it with a nod. Do not say it back upward. Otsukaresama is the safe, direction-neutral phrase that works both ways.
Back-Chain Drill — お先に失礼します
Why back-chaining works
Long phrases are easier to learn from the end backwards. The ending gets the most repetitions, so it becomes the most natural part. You'll also notice that shitsurei shimasu is already in your vocabulary — this phrase is just osaki ni added to the front.

Block C — Grammar
X は Y です
The pattern that's already inside everything you know — and that will build every sentence you learn from here.
The Big Idea
です (desu) does not mean "is." It's not a verb you translate.

Think of it this way: desu is a polite period. It signals two things at once — this sentence is now complete, and I'm being polite about it. English has no equivalent, which is why learners who try to translate it directly get confused.

Once you hear it as a sentence-ender rather than a word, the whole pattern opens up.
The Pattern

+
Y
Y
what you say about it
+
です
desu
polite period
→
As for X — it is Y.
To make it a question: add か (ka) at the end → desu ka？
Sentence Builder — Try It Yourself
How it works
Pick a topic chip, then a comment chip. Not every combination makes sense in real life — and that's part of the lesson. The builder will tell you whether your sentence is natural, grammatically fine but odd, or doesn't work.

Also notice the two name chips: 名前 (namae) is plain — used when talking about your own name or neutrally. お名前 (o-namae) adds the polite o- prefix — this form is only used when referring to someone else's name as a sign of respect toward the listener.

Block D — New Word
もう一度言ってもらえますか？
The single most useful phrase a beginner can have after greetings — and the key to avoiding nod-along miscommunication on the floor.
Why This Phrase Matters
When a Japanese colleague gives an instruction and you didn't catch it, the instinct is to nod along — because asking for a repeat feels awkward, like admitting failure. That nod-along is where real miscommunication happens.

This phrase makes it safe and respectful to ask again. And notice: onegaishimasu is already in your vocabulary from yoroshiku onegaishimasu. The ending is the same — onegai means "a request / I'm asking you."
PhraseRomaji / PhoneticMeaning
もう一度言ってもらえますか？
use this
Mou ichido itte moraemasu ka?
moh ee-chee-doh it-teh moh-reh-mas kah
 One more time, please. Polite and clear — use any time you didn't catch something.
すみません、もう一度言ってもらえますか？
softer version
Sumimasen, mou ichido itte moraemasu ka?
soo-mee-mah-sen, moh ee-chee-doh it-teh moh-reh-mas kah
 Excuse me — one more time please. The sumimasen opener softens it, especially with senior colleagues.
ゆっくり言ってもらえますか？
use this
Yukkuri itte moraemasu ka?
yook-koo-ree it-teh moh-reh-mas kah
 Could you say that more slowly? Use when they repeat at full speed — pair with mou ichido when needed.

Recovery Sequence — When You Miss Something
The situation
Someone gave instructions or introduced themselves and you didn't catch the key detail. Two phrases cover every case: Sumimasen, mou ichido itte moraemasu ka? (one more time) and Yukkuri itte moraemasu ka? (more slowly). Use mou ichido first — if they repeat at speed, follow with yukkuri.

Block E — Non-Verbal
The Bow
These phrases are almost always accompanied by a physical gesture. Knowing the words without the body language is like shaking someone's hand while looking at the floor.
Why Include This
You don't need to perform Japan. You need to not accidentally signal indifference. A small nod when you say otsukaresama, a slight bow when you say osaki ni, and a return nod when someone acknowledges you — that's it. These small gestures say "I see you, I respect this space."
🙂
What to do
A nod or shallow bow — just the head, 10–15 degrees — with otsukaresama or osaki ni is completely natural on a manufacturing floor. The gesture and the phrase together land as genuine.
↩️
Always return a bow
If someone bows or nods to you, return it. Standing straight and still when someone bows can read as cold or dismissive. A small nod back takes half a second and communicates respect.
👁️
Eye contact
Brief eye contact with a nod is warm. Sustained direct eye contact can feel intense in a Japanese interaction. Think "acknowledge and soften" rather than "hold the gaze."
🏭
Floor context
Deep formal bows aren't practical or expected on a production floor. A small head nod while saying the phrase is the norm. You're showing awareness and respect in a working context — not at a formal dinner.
